Safety Checklist

Essential steps to protect yourself and your family during a crisis.

📄 Documents & Identity

  • Keep passport and visa in a waterproof bag on your person at all times
  • Store digital copies of all documents in cloud storage (Google Drive, iCloud)
  • Share document copies with a trusted family member back home
  • Note your passport number and embassy contact on paper (in case phone dies)

📞 Communication

  • Keep your phone charged — carry a power bank at all times
  • Download offline maps of your area (Google Maps → Download offline map)
  • Set up a check-in schedule with family (e.g., every 6 hours)
  • Have a backup communication method (WhatsApp, Signal, satellite if available)

🎒 Emergency Kit

  • Water (at least 3 liters per person), non-perishable food for 72 hours
  • First aid kit with any personal medications (at least 7-day supply)
  • Cash in local currency and USD (ATMs may be down)
  • Flashlight, whistle, dust mask, and basic hygiene supplies

🏠 Shelter & Movement

  • Know the location of the nearest bomb shelter or safe room
  • Identify at least 2 evacuation routes from your current location
  • Avoid large gatherings, government buildings, and military installations
  • Keep a packed bag ready to go at all times (grab-and-go bag)

💰 Flight Prices Rising? Tips to Save

  • Search one-way tickets instead of round trips — often cheaper during crises
  • Try nearby alternative airports (AMM, LCA, CAI) — prices can be 50-70% lower
  • Use flexible date search on Google Flights to find the cheapest day
  • Check budget carriers separately (Ryanair, Wizz Air, Pegasus) — they often don't appear on aggregators
  • Book connecting flights through Istanbul (IST) or Athens (ATH) — often cheaper than direct
  • Set Google Flights price alerts — prices fluctuate hourly during crises
  • Contact your embassy — some governments arrange subsidized evacuation flights

🚨 Evacuation Routes & Border Crossings

Key land border crossings and evacuation corridors. Always verify status with your embassy before traveling.

⚠️ Border crossings can close without notice during active conflict. Always call ahead.

🇯🇴

Israel → Jordan

Allenby / King Hussein Bridge: Main crossing between West Bank and Jordan. Open to foreign nationals with valid documents. Expect long waits during crisis.

Yitzhak Rabin / Wadi Araba: Southern crossing near Eilat/Aqaba. Less congested alternative.

Jordan River / Sheikh Hussein: Northern crossing. Check status with Jordanian authorities.

🇪🇬

Israel → Egypt

Taba Crossing: Near Eilat, connects to Sinai Peninsula. Open to most nationalities. From Taba, you can reach Sharm el-Sheikh airport or continue to Cairo.

Rafah Crossing (Gaza): Controlled crossing, typically restricted. Not a viable evacuation route for most foreign nationals.

🇱🇧

Lebanon Evacuation

Beirut Airport (BEY): If operational, the fastest way out. Check airline status.

Lebanon → Syria → Turkey: Overland route via Masnaa border crossing. Extremely situation-dependent. Consult embassy.

Sea Evacuation: Some governments arrange naval evacuations from Beirut port. Register with your embassy to be included.

🇨🇾

Sea Route to Cyprus

During past conflicts, several countries organized ferry evacuations from Israeli and Lebanese ports to Larnaca, Cyprus. Cyprus is an EU member with international airport connections.

Contact your embassy about organized sea evacuations. Private charter boats may also be available from marinas.

🇹🇷

Turkey as Transit Hub

Istanbul is a major transit hub with flights to virtually every country. If you can reach Turkey (by air from nearby airports or overland from northern routes), you can connect onward.

Most nationalities can get a Turkish e-Visa online at evisa.gov.tr

📋

Before You Move

Always: Contact your embassy before attempting any border crossing. Carry all original documents. Have cash in USD and local currency. Travel during daylight. Inform family of your route and expected arrival.

Never: Approach military checkpoints without identification. Travel through active conflict zones. Rely on a single route — always have a backup plan.
